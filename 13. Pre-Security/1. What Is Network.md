## Task 1: What is Networking?
<img width="2048" height="1536" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/4c8ab72d-c84d-470b-aa1b-e68c20191b6e" />

A network is simply a group of things that are connected together.

In real life, your friends form a network because you’re connected by shared interests.
In technology, devices like phones and computers form networks to share information.

### Examples of Networks in Everyday Life

Networks are everywhere around us:
- Public transportation systems (buses, trains connected across a city)
- Power grids (electricity delivered to homes)
- Postal systems (sending letters and packages)
- Social connections (friends, neighbors)


### Networks in Computing

In computing, a network is when devices are connected to communicate.

Examples of devices in a network:
- Phones
- Laptops
- Security cameras
- Traffic lights
- Smart farming equipment

These devices send and receive data using rules (called protocols, though not detailed here).



### Size of Networks

Networks can be:

- ``Small`` → just 2 devices (like your phone and laptop)
- ``Huge`` → billions of devices (like the internet)

## Task 3: Identifying Devices on a Network

Devices need identity just like people.
- People: Name (can change) + Fingerprints (unique, permanent).
- Devices: IP Address (can change) + MAC Address (unique, fixed).

### IP Address
- Like a temporary name tag.
- It’s made of 4 numbers (octets), e.g. `192.168.1.1`
- Can change over time, but only one device in the same network can use it at once.
- Two types:
  - ``Private IP`` → used inside your home or office network.
  - ``Public IP`` → used to identify your device on the Internet.

MAC Address
- Like a fingerprint or serial number.
- Burned into the device’s network card.
- Doesn’t change (unless spoofed).

> IP = changeable name tag, MAC = permanent fingerprint.

- Private IPs:
  - Used inside your home/office network.
  - Each device (like your laptop and phone) gets its own private IP.
  - They use these to talk to each other locally.

- Public IPs:
  - Used when your devices talk to the wider Internet.
  - All devices in your home share the same public IP, because your router acts like a “gateway.” 
  - This public IP is assigned by your ISP (the company you pay for Internet).

<img width="383" height="118" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/31ece901-8fcb-4993-bcda-98e88cd33031" />

> *Note:* ISP = Internet Service Provider


- **IPv4**
  - Uses 32-bit numbers → about 4.29 billion addresses.
  - Example: ``192.168.1.1``
  - Problem: Too few addresses for billions of devices.

- **IPv6**
  - Uses 128-bit numbers → about 340 trillion trillion addresses (basically unlimited).
  - Example: 2001:0db8:85a3:0000:0000:8a2e:0370:7334
  - Benefits:
    - Solves the shortage.
    - More efficient routing and security features.

- ``IPv4``: is a small street with limited house numbers.
- ``IPv6``: is a giant city with endless house numbers, so every device can have its own unique address without running out.

### MAC Addresses

MAC Address = Device Fingerprint
- Every network card has a unique ID burned in at the factory.
- Format: 12 hexadecimal characters, split into pairs with colons (e.g., ``a4:c3:f0:85:ac:2d``).
- First half = manufacturer, second half = unique device number.

Why it matters
- Used to identify devices on a local network.
- Often used in access control (like cafés or hotels deciding which devices can connect).

Spoofing
- A device can fake its MAC address to pretend to be another.
- This can trick weak security setups — for example, if only the “admin’s MAC” is trusted, a spoofed device could sneak in.
- That’s why MAC addresses alone aren’t reliable for security.


- ``MAC`` = permanent fingerprint
- ``Spoofing`` = fake fingerprint

## Task 4: Ping (ICMP)
### Ping:
- Ping is a tool built into most operating systems (Windows, Linux, macOS).
- It uses **ICMP packets** (Internet Control Message Protocol).
- We send an **echo request** → the target replies with an echo reply.
- The time between request and reply tells you how fast/reliable the connection is.
